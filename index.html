The Opportunity

An estimated 35–45 million Filipinos with mobile phones rely on daily data sachets for messaging access, or go without entirely. Nobody sells messaging as a standalone product at any price.

Tens of millions of people buy daily data "sachets" — small prepaid bundles (₱10–20, ~$0.18–0.36) purchased at convenience stores, expiring at midnight whether used or not — just to access basic messaging apps. Sachets are popular not because monthly plans are unaffordable, but because low-income households manage money day by day — small, flexible purchases with no commitment and no account required. Over a month, sachets add up to ₱80–300 ($1.40–5.40). People are paying more for worse service, not because they have to, but because no product exists that fits how they spend.

STEP is a mobile virtual network operator (MVNO) — it leases capacity from an existing carrier's network, requiring no towers, no spectrum, and no infrastructure. STEP offers unlimited messaging for ₱28/month ($0.50) — purchased the same way, at the same stores, with cash. At ₱1/day, STEP fits within the same daily spending pattern as sachets, but costs 3.5x less than the cheapest carrier plan and 4–5x less than average sachet spending. This price point has no competitor.

The Konektadong Pinoy Act mandates fair, reasonable, non-discriminatory (FRAND) wholesale access to carrier networks. STEP has a legal right to operate. The Philippines allows 100% foreign ownership in telecommunications. The regulatory framework exists. The demand exists. Nobody has built the product.

Why This Price Works

STEP is priced for frictionless adoption — low enough that recommending STEP to a friend requires no justification. The more users adopt, the more valuable the free app becomes, and the more subscribers join. Higher pricing slows the entire loop.

₱28/month is profitable because STEP's compression technology makes messaging data negligibly small. All processing runs on-device — no cloud required. Runs on any smartphone produced.

The Market

The Philippines has 112.7 million people (2024 census) and approximately 82–85 million unique mobile subscribers (GSMA 2024). Of these, ~95% are prepaid. Carrier ARPU data confirms that the majority of prepaid users buy daily sachets rather than monthly plans — Globe's prepaid ARPU of ~₱121–138/month is consistent with purchasing ₱10–20 sachets several times a week, not ₱599+ monthly plans. The addressable market is ~35–45 million prepaid users who are already spending on messaging access through sachets but have no monthly plan. An additional 10–15 million feature phone users are transitioning to smartphones over the next 3–5 years.

The regulatory environment is actively favorable. The Konektadong Pinoy Act (RA 12234, signed August 2025) mandates FRAND wholesale access to carrier networks, establishes a streamlined registration process for new entrants, and created the legal framework for MVNOs to operate for the first time. The Philippine government wants a company like STEP to succeed — affordable messaging for tens of millions of underserved Filipinos is exactly the outcome this law was designed to produce. The implementing rules took effect in December 2025. No MVNOs currently operate in the Philippines. STEP is positioned to be the first.

Adoption rate within this market is very high. ₱28/month is 3.5x cheaper than the cheapest carrier plan and 4–5x cheaper than average sachet spending. The constraint is distribution and awareness, not willingness to switch.

Why Carriers Can't Compete

The obvious question: can't the big carriers just launch their own ₱28 messaging plan? They can't respond effectively for structural reasons:

  • They can't match the price. Globe has ~60.9M mobile subscribers, many on ₱99–₱299 plans. Launching a ₱28 messaging plan risks millions of existing subscribers downgrading — trading ₱99 revenue for ₱28. The cannibalization cost dwarfs the niche revenue. A sub-brand (like Visible by Verizon) is possible but plays into STEP's hands: it validates the market while the competing carrier burns margin to catch up — meanwhile STEP is already driving subscribers onto its partner's network.
  • They can't block STEP. The Konektadong Pinoy Act mandates FRAND wholesale access. The NTC (National Telecommunications Commission) enforces. Blocking an affordable connectivity provider is politically toxic.
  • They can't replicate the margins. Without compression, ₱28 messaging is unprofitable. STEP's 95% gross margins come from technology that carriers would need to build from scratch.
  • Carrier competition is STEP's leverage. Three carriers compete (Globe, Smart, DITO). STEP only needs one wholesale partner. The carrier who partners gains a competitive weapon against the other two — a 24M subscriber data upsell pipeline worth $43–86M/year at zero customer acquisition cost. Nobody wants their competitor to get that deal.

The Deal

$800K for 10% Class A shares. Single equity round. Shares are in a Singapore holding company that owns 100% of the Philippine operating subsidiary. Singapore provides a neutral, investor-friendly legal jurisdiction with strong corporate governance, no capital gains tax on share disposals, and an extensive tax treaty network.

  • Mandatory annual dividend: 10% of net profit (after 1.5% COO profit-share) paid to Class A shareholders, every year starting Year 2
  • Company buyback option: $12M (15x original investment), exercisable at the company's discretion. Dividends already received are kept — the buyback is independent of prior distributions.
  • Minority protections: Veto on new equity issuance, company sale, major debt, and dividend policy changes. Board observer seat. Information rights. Pro-rata rights (proportional participation) on any future offering.
  • No future equity rounds. Profitable from Year 2. Growth funded by operating profits and, if adoption outpaces projections, debt against revenue. The investor's 10% is permanent and undiluted unless buyback is exercised.
  • Founder retains 90% equity and voting control. Class B common (90%), same 1:1 voting rights as Class A. The only difference between classes is dividend: Class A receives mandatory annual dividends; Class B retains earnings in the business. Investor's minority protections (veto rights, board observer, information rights) ensure governance oversight.
